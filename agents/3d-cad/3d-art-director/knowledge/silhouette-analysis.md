# Silhouette Analysis

## 1. Why Silhouette Matters

Silhouette is the single most important design element for character recognition. Before color, before texture, before detail — the outline tells the viewer who the character is.

### Key Facts

- Players identify characters at distance by silhouette alone (minimap icons, far LOD, team recognition)
- Disney's animation test: a well-designed character should be recognizable as a solid black shape with no interior detail
- In games, characters often appear at 32-128px on screen — only silhouette reads at that scale
- A strong silhouette survives style changes (realistic to stylized) — it is the design's skeleton

## 2. The 4-Direction Silhouette Test

Every character must be tested from 4 angles. A character that only reads from the front is incomplete.

### Test Procedure

1. Set up 4 camera angles: Front (0 degrees), Side (90 degrees), Back (180 degrees), 3/4 (45 degrees)
2. Render each view with flat black fill — no lighting, no color, no interior detail
3. Evaluate each independently

### Direction-Specific Criteria

| Direction | What to Check | Common Problems |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Front** | Overall shape read, symmetry vs asymmetry, width-to-height ratio | Arms merged with body; legs appear as single column |
| **Side** | Depth read, forward/backward lean, head-to-body proportion | Flat profile — no depth in chest/belly; arms hidden behind torso |
| **Back** | Shoulder shape, hair/cape drape, tail/wing visibility | Back reads same as front (no unique elements); spine contour lost |
| **3/4 View** | The "hero angle" — most recognizable angle for most characters | Overlapping limbs; loss of negative space at this rotation |

### Scoring Per Direction

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 10 | Instantly recognizable; unique and memorable shape |
| 8-9 | Clear read; minor improvement possible |
| 6-7 | Identifiable but generic; could be multiple characters |
| 4-5 | Confusing; major elements lost or merged |
| 1-3 | Unreadable; blob or broken outline |

**Target: Minimum 7 average across all 4 directions.**

## 3. Negative Space

Negative space is the empty area within and around the character's silhouette. It is as important as the positive form.

### Rules for Negative Space

| Rule | Application |
|------|------------|
| **Limbs must have gaps** | Arms should not merge with torso in the default pose; visible daylight between arm and body |
| **Legs separated** | Even in standing pose, slight gap between legs unless intentionally wide-stance |
| **Weapon/tool reads** | A held weapon should extend the silhouette, not hide inside the body outline |
| **Hair/horns break outline** | Vertical elements (horns, tall hair) create negative space against the sky |
| **Cape/tail as negative space tool** | A flowing cape creates dramatic negative space shapes |
| **Internal gaps** | Holes in armor, gaps in clothing layers — add visual interest and break monotony |

### Negative Space Diagnostic

| Problem | Sign | Fix |
|---------|------|-----|
| "Blob" silhouette | No visible gaps anywhere | Pull arms away from body; widen stance; add protruding accessories |
| "Tube" silhouette | Character reads as a vertical column | Add horizontal elements (wide shoulders, hip sash, outstretched arm) |
| "Symmetry trap" | Left and right halves are mirrors | Introduce asymmetric element (one pauldron, offset belt, asymmetric hair) |
| "Lost limbs" | Arms/legs hidden behind body at certain angles | Adjust default pose; T-pose shows all limbs but is unnatural — use relaxed A-pose with slight arm separation |

## 4. Action Lines & Pose Dynamics

### The Line of Action

Every pose has a dominant curve (line of action) that runs through the character's body. This line defines whether the pose feels dynamic or static.

| Line Type | Shape | Feel | Use For |
|-----------|-------|------|---------|
| **Straight vertical** | I | Rigid, formal, powerful, at attention | Guards, kings, golems |
| **Slight S-curve** | S | Natural, relaxed, contrapposto | Standing characters, idle poses |
| **Strong C-curve** | C | Dynamic, leaning, about to act | Action preparation, dodging |
| **Diagonal** | / or \ | Motion, falling, attacking | Combat poses, running |
| **Extreme arc** | ( | Maximum energy, jumping, striking | Key action frames, splash art |

### Dynamic vs Static Poses

| Static Pose | Dynamic Pose |
|-------------|-------------|
| Weight evenly distributed | Weight on one leg |
| Symmetrical limb placement | Asymmetric — one arm up, one down |
| Spine straight | Spine curved or twisted |
| Head centered | Head turned or tilted |
| Shoulders level | Shoulders at different heights |
| Flat feet | Weight on ball of foot, heel lifted |

### Pose Reads in Silhouette

A dynamic pose must read in silhouette. Key test questions:
- Can you tell what the character is doing from the black shape alone?
- Does the silhouette communicate movement direction?
- Are the limbs creating clear negative space to show their position?
- Would a 5-year-old understand the action from the shadow?

## 5. Fantasy Creature Silhouette Signatures

Each fantasy race should have signature silhouette features that make it identifiable even as a distant black shape.

### Race Silhouette DNA

| Race | Signature Elements | Silhouette Shape | Key Distinction |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Goblin** | Oversized pointed ears; hunched forward lean; long arms reaching below knees | Triangular — narrow top, wider at ears/arms | Ears break outline; lean forward creates diagonal line of action |
| **Orc** | Massive shoulders; wide jaw/tusks; thick neck; heavy brow | Inverted trapezoid — very wide top, narrower at legs | Width at shoulders is dramatically wider than any other race |
| **Elf** | Pointed ears (subtle); elongated limbs; upright posture; flowing hair/cloak | Tall narrow column with flowing elements | Height + flowing lines; minimal bulk creates contrast with other races |
| **Dwarf** | Extremely broad for height; massive beard; helmet/horns; thick arms | Wide rectangle — almost as wide as tall | Width-to-height ratio closest to 1:1 of any humanoid |
| **Dragon-kin** | Wings; tail; horns; digitigrade legs; snout | Complex outline with many extending elements | Most silhouette-breaking features of any race; instantly identifiable |
| **Troll** | Hunched; long arms; small head relative to body; pot belly | Asymmetric blob — irregular outline | Hunched posture + arm length distinguishes from orc |
| **Undead** | Ragged edges; skeletal limbs; hollow areas; floating elements | Broken outline with gaps and torn shapes | The silhouette itself is damaged/incomplete — unique design language |

### Silhouette Differentiation Test

Place all races side by side as black silhouettes at the same scale. Answer:

1. Can you identify each race without labels? (Must be YES)
2. Could any two be confused for each other? (Must be NO)
3. At 64px, can you still tell them apart? (Must be YES)
4. Does each one have at least one unique outline-breaking feature? (Must be YES)

If any answer is NO, the character with the weakest silhouette needs redesign.

## 6. Silhouette Enhancement Techniques

### Adding Silhouette Interest

| Technique | How | Effect |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| **Asymmetric accessories** | One large pauldron, off-center horn, single-side cape | Breaks mirror symmetry; adds recognition |
| **Height variation** | Tall headgear, raised weapon, standing on rubble | Extends vertical silhouette beyond body |
| **Width variation** | Wide skirt, outstretched wing, shield on back | Extends horizontal silhouette |
| **Negative space holes** | Gap between arm and body, window in shield, torn cape | Creates internal shape interest |
| **Flowing elements** | Hair, cape, sash, banner, tail | Adds dynamic curves to rigid body |
| **Overlapping depth** | One arm forward, one back; one leg bent | Creates depth read even in flat silhouette |

### What Kills Silhouette

| Problem | Why It Fails |
|---------|-------------|
| All details are interior (texture, color) | Nothing reads at distance; character becomes a generic shape |
| Arms pressed against body | Limbs merge into torso; reads as pillar |
| No unique outline feature | Character could be anyone; nothing breaks the template |
| Over-complex outline | Too many small spikes/details = noise, not design |
| Symmetrical everything | No focal point; eye wanders without anchor |

## 7. Art Direction Silhouette Checklist

- [ ] 4-direction black silhouette test completed
- [ ] Each direction scores 7+ / 10
- [ ] At least 2 unique outline-breaking features visible
- [ ] Negative space present between all major limbs and body
- [ ] Character identifiable at 64px thumbnail
- [ ] Distinctly different from other characters in the same project
- [ ] Race/archetype readable from silhouette alone
- [ ] Pose communicates character personality
- [ ] Line of action is clear and appropriate
- [ ] No "blob" or "tube" silhouette problems
